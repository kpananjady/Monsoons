INDIAN MONSOON DATA

Visualizing the analysis behind monsoon headlines

2019 has been a tricky year for the Indian summer monsoon and the many that rely on it for their lives and livelihoods. What can an analysis of 145 years of data tell us about what could be happening right now?

By Kasturi Pananjady

Published July 18, 2019

It's summer in India, and anxiety about the monsoon is running high. Chennai, a highly populated South Indian state capital, suffered severe water shortages last month, a bad omen for the rest of the country. This project goes into monsoon headlines and tries to visualize the analysis that went into their creation.

In these visualizations and analyses, I'm using all India monthly rainfall data in tenths of milimeters compiled by the Indian Institute of Tropical Metereology. The data run from 1871-2016, which is great, but is in ugly pdf form, which is not so great. If you want the csv file find it in my github repo!

Some definitions. What is the monsoon?

This is an uninspired scrawl to get a quick glimpse of what rains in India have looked like over the past ten years.

Sources: Indian Institute of Tropical Metereology

From this graph, the monsoon looks charmingly regular. But while rains over a year follow general peaks and troughs, we're only really interested in the summer months.

June, July, August and September are the monsoon months, and it's their variability that we care about.

From this graph, we can see that June and September have the highest variability of any of the monsoon months, which July and August typically receive the most rain.

Sources: Indian Institute of Tropical Metereology

But this leaves out how these distributions occured across the years. Does below monthly average rain in June mean that you'll definitely have below monthly average rain in July, and so on and so forth?

From this graph, we can see that below average rain in June doesn't necessarily mean that you'll have below average rain in July. That's good news for us in 2019.

Sources: Indian Institute of Tropical Metereology

One last set of definitions: What is a drought year? What is a flood year?

The peaks above the topmost green line are floods, while the troughs under the bottom-most green line are droughts.

Sources: Indian Institute of Tropical Metereology

The red line in the middle represents the mean, and the green lines represent plus and minus 10 percent of the mean. The orange line, or the 11 year rolling mean, stays within those bounds. (More on that later.)

So what's all the hype about July rainfall?

Below average July rainfall is strongly correlated with drought. Here's June and August below, for comparison.

This is why people sometimes talk about a July 15 deadline for the monsoon to cover the whole country.

One last thing.

Notice how those drought years are clustering towards the end there? Yeah. People hypothesize that it's because of climate change, and there's been talk in the papers of reducing what is considering normal rainfall in accordance with the 10-12 rolling mean. Yeah, that orange line.

See how the orange line snakes below the middle faint red one? That's what they're talking about.
